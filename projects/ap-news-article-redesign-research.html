01 · Executive Summary

Five Headline Findings

Across 6 user interviews and 10 stakeholder interviews, five findings emerge with consistent, cross-source signal. These are not isolated complaints — they represent structural issues that affect reader experience, editorial quality, and business outcomes simultaneously.

01

The article page is trying to do too much

Ads, donation prompts, recirculation modules, newsletter sign-ups, and editorial content compete for the same space. Users and editors both describe this as the dominant problem. On a large desktop monitor, one stakeholder showed two sentences of article text visible above the fold.

02

Recirculation needs a strategy grounded in evidence

We have recirculation tools — related stories, hub peeks, inline links, third-party modules — but no unified strategy for how they work together, where they live, or what the evidence says about their effectiveness. Testing and data should drive placement and prioritization decisions, not convention.

03

AP's strongest asset — its journalism — is undersold

Users consistently cited AP's writing quality, fact-checking rigor, and reporter credibility as the reason they chose AP over competitors. None of these qualities are surfaced prominently in the article page design. Bylines, beat assignments, and in-text fact checks are buried or incidental.

04

Editorial tooling creates quality ceilings

CMS limitations — blind stacking, no scheduled publishing, broken preview, no duplicate image detection, and a broken pull quote component — mean editors cannot reliably produce the visual quality they intend. Better reader experience requires better author experience.

05

Loyal users deserve a meaningfully better experience

Our most engaged users — donors, daily readers, registered accounts — currently receive the same experience as first-time visitors. There is an opportunity to build a product that recognizes, rewards, and deepens loyalty through quality: less friction, better personalization, and a sense that AP knows who they are.

02 · Key Challenges

What Is Getting in the Way

These eight challenges were surfaced across both user and stakeholder interviews. They represent root causes, not symptoms — addressing surface-level design without resolving these will produce limited improvement.

⚡

Article element injection needs better governance

Ads, editorial modules, donations, and recirculation are injected by separate teams without a shared coordination framework. The result is articles where injected content outweighs editorial content on screen.

👁

Preview doesn't reflect production

Editors make layout decisions based on a preview that doesn't show real ads or injected modules. Layout problems are discovered only after publication.

📐

The featured template barely differs from standard

Centered content and a larger hero are the only differences. There are no full-bleed options, no text overlay, no configurable carousel or pull quote. Editors seeking more must go to the immersive team, which has limited capacity.

🔇

Push notifications are broadly rejected

The majority of interviewed users have disabled all push notifications — not just news. Email newsletters are the preferred communication channel for this cohort.

🎯

Donors are shown the same prompts as non-donors

Registered donors experience the same donation pop-ups as anonymous visitors. This is a recognition failure that actively frustrates the users most likely to advocate for AP.

🗺

Article optimization has no clear owner

Tasks like adding a video that publishes hours after a story, or ensuring related stories are accurate, fall in the gap between editorial, digital, and product — with no systematic process to catch them.

🔖

Save and bookmark are less visible and effective than they could be

Some users have built their own workarounds — emailing articles, keeping browser tabs open — because in-app save features aren't prominent enough to be trusted or discovered easily. The feature exists but its placement and promotion underserve demand.

📱

Mobile/desktop is a more useful lens than web/app

Platform decisions are framed as web vs. app, but mobile web and mobile app share a form factor. Decisions like hub peak placement should apply to both mobile contexts, not just one platform.

Both

A sticky or persistent related content module — near the top, following the reader — is the highest-confidence recirculation opportunity supported by direct evidence. Worth A/B testing on mobile.

S3 (Dave)

How the CMS and editorial workflow shape article quality — and where the current tooling creates ceilings on what editors can produce. Invisible to readers, but directly determining what readers experience.

C1
Stakeholder
There are effectively three article tiers — standard, featured, immersive — but featured barely differentiates from standard. The gap between featured and immersive is too large, and immersive capacity is extremely limited.

S1 (Christina)

How advertising and donation mechanics shape — and often degrade — the reader experience, and where there are opportunities to improve both revenue and reader trust simultaneously.

D1
Both
Ads are accepted as the cost of free content, but video ads and ads styled to mimic editorial content generate the strongest negative reactions and actively erode trust.
"The video ads just drive me nuts." / "A lot of ads look like stories we've done. It's the same exact font. Obviously it says advertisement, but I just don't love the way it looks." — User and stakeholder interviews

Full-width ads with a distinct color background (NYT-style) would reduce visual confusion while maintaining revenue. Video ads in article bodies should be deprioritized or made easier to dismiss. Ad visual differentiation is a trust issue, not just aesthetic.

05 · Appendix

Cross-Reference: Confirmations & Tensions

Where user and stakeholder findings align, confidence is high. Where they diverge, there is a design decision to make. Four of each are worth naming explicitly.

✓ Confirmed across both sources

Audio embeds are ignored

Users assume they are AI-generated. Kim independently surfaced the same insight from the editorial side. Strong signal for audio redesign — change the CTA, move it to the top, and make human authorship explicit.

↔ Tension to resolve

Recirculation vs. reading experience

Stakeholders see recirculation as the primary article goal. Users (esp. Anna) actively try not to be distracted. Resolution is position and presentation — recirculation that doesn't feel like an interruption, placed where users naturally pause.

✓ Confirmed across both sources

Ads are the primary complaint — but tolerated

Both user interviews and stakeholder feedback forms cite ad volume and presentation. Users accept ads as the cost of free content but draw the line at ads that mimic editorial content and video ads that auto-play.

↔ Tension to resolve

Personalization is desired and feared simultaneously

Users want topic surfacing but multiple users independently expressed concern about the filter bubble. Stakeholders have not surfaced this concern internally. Design must make the editorial-first model explicit in how personalization is framed and communicated.

✓ Confirmed across both sources

Related stories are sought early, not at the end

At least one user's first action was to look for related stories before reading the lead article. Stakeholder CNN data shows 20–30% engagement when related content follows the reader persistently. Consensus: move it up.

↔ Tension to resolve

Featured treatment as prestige vs. storytelling tool

Editors sometimes request featured treatment as recognition of effort ("feather in the cap") rather than because the visual assets warrant it. Dave Clark named this explicitly. Design guidance — and documented templates — can help reframe the decision around visual suitability.

✓ Confirmed across both sources

Save/bookmark demand is real, discoverability is the problem

Users have built workarounds. Stakeholders confirm the feature exists but hasn't been meaningfully promoted. This is a visibility and trust problem, not a feature gap — but it needs to be treated with the urgency of a missing feature.

↔ New from stakeholders (not surfaced by users)

CMS workflow problems are invisible to users but determine quality

Blind stacking, no scheduled publishing, broken preview, and uncoordinated injection are upstream causes of many user-facing problems. Users experience the symptoms (visual clutter, irrelevant related stories) but can't name the cause. Fixing these doesn't show up in user satisfaction immediately — but sets the ceiling for what's possible.
